Reservoir Rock Properties

Porosity:

The measure of the void (empty) space within a formation rock, expressed as a percentage or fraction of the total bulk volume.

  • Absolute Porosity: Ratio of total void space to bulk volume, regardless of connectivity.
  • Effective Porosity: Ratio of interconnected pore space to bulk volume. This is critical for production, as isolated pores cannot flow fluid.
Permeability (k):

A measure of the formation rock's ability to transmit fluids through its interconnected pore spaces. Measured in Darcies or millidarcies (mD).

HP

Pressure Fundamentals

Pressure:

The force exerted per unit area, typically measured in psi (pounds per square inch) or bar.

Hydrostatic Pressure (HP):

The pressure exerted by a column of stationary fluid at a specific true vertical depth (TVD). It is entirely dependent on fluid density and vertical height.

HP = 0.052 × Mud Weight (ppg) × TVD (ft)

Pore Pressure / Formation Pressure

The pressure exerted by the fluids (water, oil, or gas) contained within the pore spaces of the formation rock. It can be categorized as normal, subnormal, or abnormal (overpressured).

Pressure Gradients

Definition: The rate at which pressure increases with vertical depth. In well control, it is commonly expressed in psi/ft (API).

🌊 1. Normal Formation Pressure

IWCF Definition: A pressure equal to the hydrostatic pressure of a full column of native formation fluid extending from the depth of the formation to the surface.

The Mechanism: The pore spaces are connected all the way to the surface (open system). The fluids within the pores only support the weight of the fluid column above them.

Standard IWCF Values:
  • • Freshwater: 0.433 psi/ft (8.33 ppg)
  • • Saltwater: 0.465 psi/ft (8.94 ppg)
Operational Rule: Drill safely with MW slightly above native (e.g., 9.0 - 9.2 ppg) to provide a standard trip margin.

💥 2. Abnormal Formation Pressure (Overpressure)

IWCF Definition: Any formation pore pressure that is greater than the normal hydrostatic pressure of the formation water for that specific TVD.

The Mechanism: Requires a sealing mechanism (cap rock or impermeable barrier) and a pressurizing mechanism. Trapped fluid is forced to support a portion of the overlying rock matrix (the overburden).

Primary Causes (IWCF Syllabus Focus):
  • Under-compaction of Shales (Most Common): Trapped water bears rock weight.
  • Artesian Effect: Formation outcrop is higher than rig floor.
  • Gas Cap Effect: Low-density gas transmits high bottom pressure to the top.
  • Faulting / Salt Domes: Fluid migration or blocked pathways.

📉 3. Subnormal Formation Pressure

Pore pressure that is less than the normal hydrostatic pressure for that vertical depth.

Primary Causes:
  • Depletion: The most common cause in modern drilling. Hydrocarbons produced from reservoir artificially lower the pressure.
  • Thermal Contraction: Cooling of formation fluids.
⚠️ Operational Risk: Severe lost circulation. Standard mud weights will be heavily overbalanced.

Overburden Pressure

The pressure exerted by the total weight of the overlying rock matrix and the fluids contained within its pore spaces at a specific depth. It represents the absolute maximum stress at any given point.

Bottom Hole Pressure (BHP)

BHP is the total pressure exerted at the bottom of the wellbore. Its value changes depending on whether the fluid is static or moving.

🛑
Static BHP:

The pressure at the bottom of the well when the mud pumps are off and the fluid is not moving.

BHP = HP
🌊
Dynamic BHP (Circulating):

The pressure at bottom when pumps are on. Fluid friction moving up the annulus adds pressure.

BHP = HP + Annular Pressure Loss

The Choke vs. Bell Nipple Difference

Circulating to Bell NippleOpen System

The fluid returns to surface at atmospheric pressure. Dynamic BHP is solely HP plus Annular Friction.

↓↓
Circulating through ChokeClosed System

Returning fluid is forced through the choke, applying backpressure.
Dynamic BHP = HP + Friction + Choke Backpressure.

Well Control Surface Pressure Limits

💥 MAASPMaximum Allowable Annular Surface Pressure

The maximum pressure that can be applied to the annulus at the surface (with the BOP closed) before the formation at the casing shoe breaks down. Assumes a static fluid column.

MAASP = (Frac MW - Current MW) × 0.052 × Shoe TVD
⚠️ MASPMaximum Anticipated Surface Pressure

The highest pressure predicted to be encountered at surface during a specific well operation (e.g., a worst-case gas kick reaching surface). Heavily used in equipment design and selection.

⚙️ MAWPMaximum Allowable Working Pressure

The maximum safe working pressure of a specific piece of equipment (e.g., BOP, choke manifold, valves) as rated by the manufacturer. Equipment must never be operated above its MAWP.

Circulating Friction Losses

During circulation, mud pump pressure is entirely consumed by friction as fluid travels through the system.

APL (Annular Pressure Loss)

Pressure required to push fluid up the annulus from bit to surface.

Surface Losses

Friction through standpipe, kelly hose, top drive, and surface piping.

Drill String & BHA Losses

Friction through the inside of drill pipe, HWDP, drill collars, and tools.

Bit Losses

The massive pressure drop across the bit nozzles to maximize hydraulic impact.

Key Distinctions in the System

🥇
Where is the highest friction loss?

➔ The Bit.

A significant portion of the total pump pressure (often 50% or more) is intentionally dropped across the bit nozzles to maximize hydraulic impact and clean the hole.

🎯
Which part is most important for well control?

➔ The Annulus (APL).

While it accounts for a very small percentage of the total system pressure loss, it acts directly on the open hole. APL increases the dynamic BHP, creating Equivalent Circulating Density (ECD). If APL is too high, it can fracture the formation; if suddenly removed (shutting off pumps), the pressure drop can allow a kick.

⚖️ Dynamic Pressure Mechanics

Primary well control is dynamic. While static Hydrostatic Pressure (HP) is key, turning the mud pumps ON adds friction pressure—Equivalent Circulating Density (ECD)—to the bottom of the hole. Notice how turning pumps OFF removes ECD and can instantly trigger a kick if the static mud weight is too low.

⚙️ Enrichment Module: Managed Pressure Drilling (MPD)

Modern onshore drilling often utilizes Managed Pressure Drilling (MPD) to overcome narrow pressure windows. During MPD operations, the Bottom Hole Pressure (BHP) is kept constant by dynamically applying or relieving surface backpressure via an automated choke.

If an influx occurs during MPD, the system can instantly increase backpressure to halt the influx without physically shutting in the BOP. This maintains real-time downhole pressure data and minimizes the influx volume.

📘 Advanced Enrichment: Tripping Mechanics & Pumping Out

Statistically, the majority of well control incidents occur during tripping. Swab and surge pressures are dictated by pipe speed, mud viscosity, and annular clearance.

Rigorous Volumetric Monitoring

  • Monitor displacement strictly every 5 stands (Drill Pipe), 3 stands (HWDP), and 1 stand (Drill Collars).
  • Stripping Tanks & Agitation: Use a dedicated stripping tank (calibrated to 0.02 bbl/inch) for ultra-fine measurement during stripping. Ensure trip tanks use paddle agitators to prevent barite sag and maintain mud density consistency.
  • Prohibited: Never use a centrifugal pump to continuously fill from the suction pit. Accurate measurement is impossible.

String Configuration & "Pumping Out"

  • Bit Balling & Closed Strings: Reduces annular clearance, acting like a piston and multiplying swabbing suction forces drastically.
  • Pumping Out: For complex onshore wells, slim holes, or when using high density/viscosity mud, swabbing risk is extreme. The best practice is to perform POOH (Pulling Out Of Hole) while simultaneously pumping mud.

🕵️‍♂️ Onshore Masking Factors

A "False Negative." Routine onshore operations cause fluctuations in pit volume or flow rate that can hide an influx, causing crews to ignore positive indicators.

  • 🔀
    Mud Transfers (Active/Reserve)Transferring fluid between pits bypasses total volume tracking.
  • ⚖️
    Mud Additions & DilutionAdding barite/chemicals alters pit levels, masking slow gains from a kick.
  • 🚿
    Pump Start/Stop & Drain BackFlowline drain back and ECD changes cause temporary volume surges in pits.
  • ⚙️
    Solids Control & SpillsDumping from shakers/centrifuges or surface leaks mask volume gains.
Important Definitions:
  • Drain Back: Mud draining from the flowline and surface equipment back into the active pits after pumps are stopped, causing a temporary, false pit volume increase.
  • Flow Return: The normal return of mud from the wellbore. Once pumps are stopped and drain back completes, flow returns must cease. Any continuous flow return is a positive kick indicator.

🎈 Ballooning vs. True Kick

Ballooning occurs when dynamic ECD forces mud into formation micro-fractures. When pumps turn off, rock squeezes mud back out.

Rule: Ballooning flow is FINITE (slows down and stops). Kick flow is CONTINUOUS.

📡 Enrichment Module: Automated Early Kick Detection (EKD)

While conventional monitoring relies on human observation and standard Pit Volume Totalizers (PVT), modern onshore rigs are increasingly adopting advanced EKD systems to reduce the influx volume before shut-in. Industry studies (including onshore Texas regression analyses) demonstrate that 54% of kick-triggering causes can be mitigated by implementing automated EKDS and MPD technologies.

Conventional PVTDetection threshold: 10+ barrels. Heavily reliant on visual float levels and highly susceptible to masking factors.
Coriolis Flow MetersDetection threshold: 3-5 barrels. Measures mass flow rate out vs. in, providing a 50% improvement in response time.
Downhole MWD SensorsDetection threshold: Instantaneous. Detects annular pressure anomalies at the bit milliseconds after they occur.

🚨 The Action Protocol

Upon observing a positive indicator, immediately execute the Flow Check and Shut-In procedures.

1

Stop Rotary Table

Halt drilling rotation to assess without inducing dynamic pressures.

2

Pick Up Off Bottom

Raise drill string so tool joint is positioned above the BOP rams.

3

Stop Mud Pumps

Shut down active circulation to remove friction pressure (ECD).

4

Observe & Shut In

Flow Check. If flowing with pumps off, ACTIVATE BOP IMMEDIATELY.

📋 Enrichment Module: API Well Control Drills & Non-Routine Operations (Onshore)

Drills must be conducted regularly to train crews in rapid barrier reinstatement per API/IADC standards. Reaction times must be recorded.

🕳️

Pit Drill (Drilling)

Frequency: Every 14 days / entering HP zones
  1. Recognize simulated kick (alarm).
  2. Hoist Kelly/Top Drive to space out tool joint.
  3. Stop mud pumps.
  4. Shut the well in (Annular or Rams).
🏗️

Trip Drill (Tripping)

Frequency: Every 14 days
  1. Recognize anomaly & set stand down on slips.
  2. Install FOSV (open position).
  3. Close FOSV and close BOP.
  4. Make up Top Drive / Inside BOP.
  5. Open FOSV & HCR to choke line.

Other Drills

Choke Manipulation Drill

Conducted strictly before drilling out shoe tracks. Discharge into trip tank to accurately monitor flow rates and pressure drops.

Stripping Drill

Conducted after BOP test. Involves running drill pipe to bottom under pressure through a closed annular preventer.

Non-Routine Ops & Casing (Onshore)

Two-Barrier Policy

During wireline logging, running perforated liners, or setting slips without packers, two independent barriers must be strictly maintained at all times to prevent sudden influxes.

Wireline & Intervention

Wireline pressure control equipment (lubricators, wireline BOPs, and grease injection systems) must be rigorously pressure-tested to allow safe wellbore access without losing primary control.

Casing Running Best Practices

Perform a short trip to eliminate tight spots. Condition the mud by reducing Plastic Viscosity (PV) and Yield Point (YP) to minimize surge pressures. Circulate at least 120% of casing capacity before cementing.

          🌳 The Kick Detection Decision Tree

          A warning sign means the well might be going underbalanced. A positive indicator means the well is actively kicking.

          As per IWCF, the absolute first action to take when a Kick Warning Sign (such as a sudden drilling break, increase in background gas, or change in cuttings) is observed is to stop operations and perform a Flow Check.

          Immediate Action: The Flow Check Procedure (IWCF Standard)

          You do not immediately shut in a well based only on a warning sign. Instead, you verify if the well is flowing statically. When a warning sign is observed, the Driller must immediately execute the following sequence:

          1. Stop Rotating: Cease rotation and remove Weight on Bit (WOB).
          2. Pick Up Off Bottom: Pick up the drill string so that the bit is off the bottom. Crucial: Ensure a tool joint is positioned above the rotary table.
          3. Stop the Pumps: Turn off the mud pumps to remove Equivalent Circulating Density (ECD).
          4. Observe the Well: Route the flow line to the trip tank and observe the well for flow.

          The Decision Tree (Post-Flow Check)

          Once the pumps are off and the well is static, the Driller has two paths based on their observation:

          🚨 Scenario A: The Well is Flowing (Positive Indicator)

          If fluid continues to flow out of the wellbore with the pumps off, the warning sign has escalated to a Positive Kick Indicator.

          → Action: Immediately sound the alarm and execute the Hard Shut-In Procedure.

          🛑 Scenario B: The Well is NOT Flowing (Static)

          If the well is completely dead with the pumps off, a kick is not actively entering the wellbore.

          → Action: Do not simply resume drilling at full speed. Circulate Bottoms Up through the choke manifold.

          ⭐
          The Golden Rule

          Warning Sign → Stop & Flow Check. Only shut in if the flow check is POSITIVE.
